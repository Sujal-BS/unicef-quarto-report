Vitamin A Supplementation: Saving Children's Lives Worldwide

Sujal Boodgere Sunil - A00096721 | BAA1030 UNICEF Data Analyst Report

Executive Summary

Vitamin A is essential for healthy vision, immune function, and growth in children. Deficiency in this vital nutrient is a major public health issue, especially in low-income countries. It can lead to preventable blindness and increases the risk of death from common infections like measles and diarrhoea.

Fortunately, simple Vitamin A supplementation twice a year can dramatically improve child survival. Studies show it can reduce mortality by up to 23% in children aged 6 to 59 months.

This report examines global coverage of Vitamin A supplementation, its link with economic factors, and its impact on child mortality using the latest UNICEF data.

Key Findings

  • Many countries have made good progress in coverage since 2000, but huge gaps still exist between rich and poor nations.
  • Countries with higher GDP per capita generally achieve much better supplementation rates.
  • Several countries continue to have coverage below 10%, leaving millions of children at serious risk.
  • The COVID-19 pandemic caused a clear drop in coverage in 2020 across many regions.
  • Low coverage is often linked to higher child mortality rates between ages 5 and 9.

Recommendations

To save more children’s lives, the following steps are recommended:

  • Strengthen supply chains so supplements reach even remote areas reliably.
  • Combine Vitamin A distribution with regular vaccination drives and nutrition programmes.
  • Run community awareness campaigns so parents understand the importance of supplementation.
  • Provide extra support and funding to countries with coverage below 50%.

Small improvements in coverage can lead to big reductions in child suffering and death.
